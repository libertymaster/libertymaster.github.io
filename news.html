    Money Creation and the Hyperinflation Conspiracy

    In The New York Times  column entitled  “ Money Makes Crazy ”  by Paul Krugman, the author claims that the understanding concerning monetary policies acknowledged by many Republicans is, “crazy — full-on conspiracy-theory crazy.” Mr. Krugman’s argument is based on his assertion that a particular viewpoint is “crazy,” or even conspiratorial, and Krugman is completely wrong about the Federal Reserve, hyperinflation, and his assertion that only Republicans view Fed policy as dangerous to the economy.

    Krugman states in his thesis: “So it matters that the emerging G.O.P. consensus on money is crazy — full-on conspiracy-theory crazy” (Krugman). Using words like “money madness,” Krugman then goes on to attack Rand Paul and other Republican politicians who are critical of the Fed’s policies (Krugman). Krugman contends that since the Fed is immune from bank runs, hyperinflation is a silly fantasy (Krugman). The author cites “the conservative worldview,” as he articulates in the column, which he claims shapes these “crazy” ideas about monetary questions because of a bias in favor of the wealthy (Krugman). One illogical fallacy presented by Krugman is ad hominem. In an attack, Krugman negatively labels people for their personally drawn conclusions regarding the monetary system, yet provides no real evidence to support his claims. Krugman’s reasoning could also be construed as stereotyping, and presenting a red herring as well. Furthermore, evidence can be presented that contradicts Krugman’s claims that only Republicans hold these “crazy” views regarding money. As reported by the International Business Times in an article entitled, “Federal Reserve Advisory Committee Worries About Inflation, ‘Unsustainable Bubble’ In Stocks And Bonds” by Sreeja VN, the author describes how board members on advisory committees with Fed members are voicing concerns over out of control inflation due to long term quantitative easing policies from the Fed. Another example of inflation and hyperinflation being discussed as a possible consequence of quantitative easing can be found in a CNBC article entitled,  “ Hyperinflation … in Japan?” by Mia Tahara-Stubbs. Here the author writes about a growing concern for rising prices and possible hyperinflation from consumers in Japan who cite the Bank of Japan’s quantitative easing policy as the possible culprit for the increases (Stubbs). There are many resources available concerning banking, inflation, and hyperinflation, including publications demonstrating the history and realities of monetary matters.

    For example, Cato Institute’s Working Paper  entitled, “World Hyperinflations,” elaborates upon fifty-six examples of hyperinflation, mostly throughout the twentieth century. This demonstrates that hyperinflation is not a fictitious notion, but a recorded fact found within history. Forbes Magazine published an article entitled, “Is U.S. Hyperinflation Imminent?” by Mike Patton. In this piece Patton states, “However, since inflation and hyperinflation are triggered by an excess of currency, which is not backed by gold or any other substance of value   (i.e.; called “fiat” currency), there is no limit to the amount of dollars the U.S. can print…” (Patton). This is an extremely important contention from a very reliable publication. Inflation and hyperinflation have been documented throughout history, and can devastate an economy.

    In conclusion, Krugman is playing cheerleader for the Fed, and all the while the Fed board members are criticizing their own agency within their own committee, and for the same quantitative easing policies of which Rand Paul has spoken out against. Krugman can claim Rand Paul has “off the wall” ideas concerning the monetary system, yet the economist may one day be forced to acknowledge that history was on Mr. Paul’s side. Repeating the stereotype of “crazy” and “conspiracy” to argue against a particular position on monetary policy, or any issue for that matter, only does the individual a disservice and completely weakens their argument. By attacking certain Republicans, and not presenting any substantive evidence or facts to the reader, Krugman’s argument falls flat on its illogical foundation.
