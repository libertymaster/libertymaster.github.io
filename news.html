Surveillance and the Fourth Amendment

November 6, 2014 by Lamont Michael Kimberlin

Concerning his National Security Agency revelations, Edward Snowden said,

I don't see myself as a hero, because what I'm doing is self-interested. I don't want to live in a world where there's no privacy, and therefore no room for intellectual exploration and creativity.

As referenced by David H. Price in his composition entitled, "The New Surveillance Normal: NSA and Corporate Surveillance in the Age of Global Capitalism," Price communicates a large shif that occurred in public opinion concerning comprehensive government eavesdropping on Americans after September 11, 2001. In the three decades prior to 9/11, opposition to extensive government eavesdropping by those surveyed fluctuated between 70-80% (Price). Yet, The New York Times poll conducted in December of 2001 found that only 44% considered comprehensive surveillance as infringing upon the rights of Americans (Price). In a world that is shrinking by the day due to advancements in technology and an ever-expanding surveillance state, some people are taking notice and seeking to protect their rights to privacy.

Citizens who care about their rights to privacy must speak out and urge their government to honor their Fourth Amendment rights to avoid a future of oppression and suppression which will affect generations to come. When Edward Snowden released intelligence concerning some of the details regarding the National Security Agency’s vast surveillance system, a rippling effect of contention circulated around the world, and people began to examine the surveillance apparatus more closely (Price). Other government agencies, such as the CIA, have come under scrutiny as well. Former CIA agent Philip Agee characterizes the function of the NSA and CIA as

Their function is essentially the secret police of U.S. capitalism, plugging up leaks in the political dam night and day.

Many would argue that unchecked and secret power usurps the rights of the people.

Blowback and a Failed American Foreign Policy

April 5, 2015 by Lamont Michael Kimberlin

The foreign policy of the United States has not only produced death, starvation, and increased chaos around the world; but it has also created a system by which irrational and irresponsible programs are carried out in the name of spreading democracy (Johnson). For example, hundreds of pounds of one-hundred dollar bills were sent to Iraq and distributed by American soldiers to Iraqi fighters, including fighters partnered or associated with al-Qaeda (Jasper).

American foreign policy architects, including those elected and appointed, must put an end to the bribes, corrupt activity, and secret operations which shape U.S. foreign policy, and directly contribute to the deaths and injuries of hundreds of thousands of American soldiers and innocent civilians, while endangering the national security of America. These responsible parties must replace these failed and atrocious policies with a foreign policy based on diplomacy and peace by leading the world through an exceptional example, and only using lethal force in the case of national defense. It is crucial to understand the term “blowback” in order to comprehend the scope and impact of these chain of events set in motion by foreign military and covert interventions.
